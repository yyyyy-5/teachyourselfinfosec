Teach Yourself Information Security 1.0

Inspired by Teach Yourself Computer Science, this guide intends to provide a basic set of resources for getting started with learning computer security while also answering the following questions:

  • Which subjects should you learn, and why?
  • What are the best books / video series / CTF challenges for each subject?

tl;dr:

The consensus is that to build a strong foundation for infosec, you will want to start by studying networking, operating systems fundamentals (Windows & Linux), and some sort of scripting/programming language like Python or Bash. Study these first, and then dive into another subject (some of which are listed here) that piques your interest, if you want. There are many domains of infosec to explore.

The most important skill

Being able to research effectively is the most important skill that a learner can have. No one knows everything, you will inevitably forget things, and as your knowledge/experience increases, you will inevitably need to research questions that do not have a straightforward answer.

Bottom line: If you know how to find the answer to a question, you know the answer to the question.

Basic research guide coming soon, but some quick tips in the meantime:

  • Google search operators allow you to look for data on specific sites, in specific formats (e.g. "webshell" site:github.com file:.aspx will return ASPX webshells on Github.com)
  • Typing the man or help command on Linux/Unix and Windows (respectively) before a command will give you more information on the arguments the command takes and how to use it.
  • If there a specific domain you're interested in, Google awesome <domain> (e.g. 'awesome networking' or 'awesome osint'). This will bring up a lot of resources related to the topic you're interested in, typically in a Github repo.

Networking

Resources:

  • Professor Messer's Network+ Videos cover a broad range of networking topics, and is structured around the objectives of the Comptia Network+ exam.
    • You can use certification guides as general subject study guides, without taking the exam (which costs money). A lot of IT certifications are glorified vocabulary tests that try to hit as many relevant points as possible, so they're excellent for rapidly understanding the basics.

Operating Systems

Resources:

  • The Elements of Computing Systems (also called Nand2Tetris) is a book at will take you from building NAND logic gates to an assembler to programming a game (like Tetris) in a high-level language, giving you a pretty comprehensive understanding of how a computer works. It is project-centric, and there is also a 2-part Coursera course (free to audit) with video instruction.

Programming

Focus on understanding how to program with sockets, and understand how to make network connections with your scripts. This is the most relevant Resources:

  • Harvard's CS50 OpenCourseWare (free online course) takes students through fundamental computer science and programming concepts using multiple languages. It encorporates projects and is also available as an EdX MOOC course.
  • Automate the Boring Stuff with Python is a (free) book that focuses on practical, hands-on scripting. Of particular interest is the Web Scraping chapter.

Cryptography

Cryptography involves understanding quite a bit of math. If you don't have a strong foundation in math, consider reading / skimming Mathematics for Computer Science, a free MIT OpenCourseWare textbook that has an accompanying online course. You can skip around. Reading through the Number Theory section is recommended for Cryptography, specifically.

Resources:

  • Dan Boneh's Online Cryptography Course provides an amazing overview of cryptography and includes videos and a free textbook (which comes with proofs and homework exercises). You can also take the course on the Coursera MOOC platform.
  • [CryptoHack] is an online CTF platform focused around learning cryptography, focusing on "breaking bad implementations of "modern" crypto, such as AES, RSA, and Elliptic-curve" in an accessible way. Knowledge of a programming language (especially Python) is highly recommended. Some of the problems, like the 'Crypto on the Web' problems are very practical.

Malware Analysis

A prerequisite for malware analysis is setting up an environment where you can safely detonate and analyze malware, and reset the environment back to 'normal' once you're done. This is generally done through the use of virtual machines (VMs), which are essentially emulated computers that you can run from your own machine (host OS) or the cloud (You can also use Docker containers). FireEye's FLARE VM is a Windows 10 environment that comes with a ton of preinstalled tools for both dynamic and static analysis. Note that you will need a Windows 10 ISO for it. REMnux is a Linux-based toolkit for malware analysis, and is especially helpful for analyzing malicious documents (maldocs)

  • See VirtualBox's Setup Guide for instructions on setting up a free hypervisor (which allows you to run VMs on your host ccomputer) and info on how hypervisors work.

Resources:

  • Practical Malware Analysis is a book that takes students through the basics of static and dynamic analysis, basic disassembly, and malware functionality. Each chapter has a set of labs which are downloadable via the book website.
    • The amazing Sam Bowne periodically hosts an online PMA course (using the textbook and going through the projects) which he graciously allows any student to audit online for free. Check his website to see if he's teaching it soon, or use the videos/lecture notes from a previous iteration.

Resources for Researching Malware:

  • Malpedia is pretty invaluable for tracking malware and associated threat actor groups. The site acts as an aggregator for information on different groups and malware families, providing easy access to high quality information on a specific type of malware you're trying to research.
  • vx-underground groups malware sample by family and APT (advanced persistent threat) groups, with no account needed to download samples.
  • ThreatFox IoC Database is constantly being updated with indicators of compromise (e.g. hashes, domain names, IP addresses, etc) for malware samples. Search by IoC, tag, or malware family

Web App Exploitation

Resources:

  • The OWASP Top 10 represents the most widespread / common web application vulnerabilities and is a standard for web application security. Click the hamburger menu (≡) > Top 10:2021 List to navigate to different pages explaining each vulnerability.
  • PortSwigger Web Security Academy is a free self-guided set of labs/guides that explains web application vulnerabilies, providing hands-on labs for exploitation using the Burp Suite Framework. Note that for some labs, using the Burp Suite Professional (not free) is advised - you can skip these labs or complete (some of) them with Burp Suite Community (free) Edition (which is admittedly a bit harder).

What do I do about my imposter syndrome?

Imposter syndrome involves feeling like you're incompetent and will inevitably fail, despite your experience / education / skills / accomplishments.

What to do about this? Practice failing. When you have imposter syndrome, the idea of failing can cause you to feel paralyzed, or make you overreact when you make a mistake. When you practice failing, you realize that making mistakes is inevitable. Failure is fleeting, but so is success. When you go into things with the mindset of doing your best, but accepting that you may not be perfect, you become resilient to failure.

We've all accidentally mis-triaged something or accidentally brought down a production server for a few hours. It doesn't make you a bad or stupid or careless person. While making a mistake or miss does not have any bearing on who you are, the way you recover from the miss does. At the end of the day, people are too busy thinking/worrying about themselves, and will not remember you making a mistake. People WILL remember you if you:

  • show self-awareness by analyzing your process
  • show passion by caring about your mistake
  • show resilience and humility by overcoming your anxiety/frustration and using the mistake as a learning opportunity
  • show leadership by using your mistake as a chance to teach others the lessons you learned

A good way to practice failing is to try something new or difficult with other people (or with a mentor) and mess up in front of them. Fail to do the really hard CTF challenge and ask for help. Push through the paralysis that sets in when you've realized you've started a very bad drawing, finish it, and show it to someone. Allow yourself to not do things perfectly.

Also, write down your 'wins' - the things you have accomplished and are proud of - so that when you make a mistake, you can remember that you've also done awesome things and will continue to both excel and fuck up in the future. It is inevitable.
